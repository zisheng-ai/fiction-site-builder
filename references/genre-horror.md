# Genre: Horror

Load this file alongside `story-long-write.md` when writing horror fiction of any sub-genre.

## Horror Sub-genres

| Sub-genre | Primary fear axis | AdSense notes |
|---|---|---|
| Survival Horror | Physical threat, attrition, resource scarcity | Safe — no gore required; keep wounds functional not gratuitous |
| Psychological Horror | Perception failure, identity dissolution, gaslighting | Safe — the most AdSense-compatible sub-genre |
| Cosmic / Lovecraftian Horror | Insignificance, incomprehension, the meaninglessness of survival | Safe — threat is conceptual, not physical |
| Haunted House Gothic | Violated domestic safety, place as antagonist, past trauma made manifest | Safe — keep supernatural not visceral |
| Creature Horror | Unknown biology, predatory intelligence, the wrong number of limbs | Safe if threat is shown by effect; flag if gore-adjacent creature description is considered |
| Domestic Horror | The familiar turned enemy, intimate betrayal, the monster in the home | Safe — most disturbing sub-genre that is also the cleanest for policy |
| Body Horror | Physical transformation, loss of bodily autonomy, wrongness of flesh | **AdSense risk** — graphic body horror with detailed gore description risks policy violation; keep transformation implied or psychological rather than anatomically detailed |
| Supernatural Thriller | Threat with momentum, countdown structure, horror + investigation hybrid | Safe — thriller pacing contains horror elements within a forward-moving plot |

## The Horror Click Standard

Horror readers buy three things simultaneously: **FEAR** (immediate, physical), **MYSTERY** (what is this thing?), and **SURVIVAL STAKES** (can she get out?). A scene with only atmosphere is mood. A scene with only chase is action thriller. A scene with only mystery is detective fiction. Horror requires all three active — the reader must feel afraid of something specific that they don't fully understand while caring whether the protagonist survives.

The three must be present simultaneously, not sequentially. A chapter that builds atmosphere for five pages, then introduces the threat, then reveals the stakes has not achieved horror — it has achieved three separate beats. The test: at any sentence in a horror scene, the reader should be able to answer yes to "is she in danger?", "does she not fully understand what is happening?", and "does the reader care if she gets out?" If any answer is no, the scene is running on fewer than its three cylinders.

**The AdSense floor:** Horror must disturb, not disgust. The distinction is where the sensation lands — disturb registers in the mind and the back of the neck; disgust registers in the stomach. A reader who is disturbed keeps reading. A reader who is disgusted closes the tab and does not return. Keep the threat's horror in what it means and what it does, not in the anatomical detail of how it does it.

## Named Emotional States (Horror)

| State | Internal contradiction | How it reads on the page |
|---|---|---|
| **Hyperawareness** | She is treating every sensory input as a potential threat; her processing is faster than she can speak; the world has contracted to immediate radius | Short percussive sentences; no abstract thought; each sensory detail named and evaluated in sequence; the body conducting triage faster than the mind can follow |
| **Contaminated safety** | A space that was safe no longer is; she cannot locate where the new boundary falls; nowhere she currently stands is certain | She checks the ordinary things — the locked door, the light switch — and finds they are still working, which is somehow worse; she cannot find the source of wrongness, only its edges |
| **Compelled dread** | She knows she should not go forward; she goes forward; she cannot explain why even to herself | First-person narration splits: she reports what her body is doing as if observing it from outside; "I walked toward the door" without interiority; the compulsion reads as dissociation from her own will |
| **Dissociation under threat** | The mind removes itself from the body's experience; she observes from a slight distance | Clinical observation of her own actions; short declarative sentences with no emotional coloring; the body does things without instructions; the absence of internal monologue is the signal |
| **Grim resolve** | She has passed fear and arrived somewhere colder; there is no choice left to evaluate; she moves | Zero interiority; single-task sentences; the emotional content of this register is zero, which is its own kind of horror — the reader understands she has spent all her fear and arrived somewhere past it |
| **False relief** | She believes it is over; the reader knows it is not | Write the specific way her body relaxes — shoulders, breath, the loosening of the jaw; name the wrong things as safe; the reader's dread in this moment comes from knowing what she does not |
| **Witnessed wrongness** | She has seen something that has rewritten the texture of everything else; the world looks the same; it no longer is | Everything she describes now carries a second layer; the ordinary detail of the kitchen, the street, another person's face — all of it has changed in meaning without changing in appearance |

## Fear Escalation — The Terror Register

This is horror's primary escalation axis. Manage it as deliberately as heat in romance — the reader tracks it unconsciously, and a chapter that drops the register without structural reason loses them.

### Target Fear Level

| Level | Definition | Policy note | When to use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Unease** | Something is wrong; not yet definable; protagonist dismisses it; reader is ahead of her | Fully safe | Ch 1–4; establish the ordinary world before contaminating it |
| **Dread** | The threat is real and approaching; she knows this now; the only question is when | Fully safe | Ch 4–(N-4); sustained dread is the spine of the novel |
| **Terror** | The threat is immediate; no safe position exists; survival is uncertain; the body takes over from the mind | Safe — keep physical violence functional, not gratuitous | Ch (N-3)–(N-2); the climax approach |
| **Horror** | The full nature of the threat is revealed; what she feared was less than what it is; the reveal is worse than the approach | Safe if the reveal is conceptual, psychological, or implication-based; flag if anatomically graphic | Final chapters; this is the genre's payoff beat |

### The 4 Fear Techniques

**1. The known unknown**

Name the effect, never the cause. *Something moved in the corner* is more frightening than *a creature crouched in the corner*. The gap between observed effect and unnamed cause is where fear lives — the reader's imagination fills it with exactly what they most fear. The instant the cause is named, the reader's imagination is replaced by the writer's description, which is almost always less frightening.

The technique has a lifespan. In early chapters, effect-without-cause is maximally effective because the reader's uncertainty is total. By the mid-book, the reader needs partial reveals to sustain engagement — the complete unknown becomes frustrating rather than frightening. Feed one specific wrong detail per partial reveal: the angle that is impossible, the smell that shouldn't be there, the sound it makes when it is very close. Each detail should be genuinely specific and strange — not "it smelled wrong" but the actual wrong smell. Keep the worst detail last.

When to finally name the cause: at or after the climax. Even then, consider whether naming improves or diminishes. Psychological and cosmic horror almost always diminish on full explanation. Creature horror and domestic horror usually improve — the full revelation of what was actually in the house lands harder when the reader has been dreading it. Make the choice based on what the threat is: if the threat is *an entity*, name it; if the threat is *a concept or a force*, withhold.

**2. The false alarm → real threat**

Horror conventions have trained readers to expect that most creaks are the house settling and most shadows are tricks of light. The false alarm is not a failure of craft — it is a structural tool. Used correctly, it lowers the reader's guard so the real event lands harder.

Rules: Use at most one deliberate false alarm before the real event. Two false alarms feel like the writer stalling. The false alarm must be convincing — it should genuinely relax both the protagonist and the reader; a false alarm the reader doesn't believe produces no relief and therefore no contrast. The false alarm should have a reasonable explanation that the protagonist accepts. The real threat arrives when the reader has just exhaled.

The mechanics: false alarm → protagonist checks → reasonable explanation → she accepts, relaxes → the real event uses the same beat (the same creak, the same shadow, the same door) — but this time the explanation is not there. The repetition of the structure is the technique. She checks exactly as she did before. The answer is different.

**3. Sound as the primary dread carrier**

Sound carries dread more reliably than sight in prose because the reader cannot see the page — they hear it. A description of what something looks like requires the reader to construct an image. A description of what something sounds like bypasses construction and arrives directly as sensation. This is horror's built-in advantage.

The three sound patterns:

*The wrong sound:* a sound that exists where it shouldn't, or at an hour when it shouldn't, or with a quality it shouldn't have. The footsteps on the floor above in an empty house. The breathing on the other end of the phone after the call ended. The child's voice from the room you just locked. What makes these land is specificity — not "a sound" but the exact sound, its rhythm, its source direction, whether it is getting louder or not.

*The wrong silence:* the sound that stops precisely when she listens for it. The birds that go quiet. The hum that was there for three chapters and is absent now. The silence where there should be breath. The stopped sound is more frightening than the wrong sound because the reader understands that something heard her listening.

*The sound that reveals:* the tell that comes before full exposure. The dragging from the basement. The rhythm that almost sounds like words. The pattern she recognizes on the third repetition as something deliberate. The sound that reveals is the bridge between unease and dread — she can no longer explain it away.

Write sounds with specificity and restraint. One specific wrong sound per scene. Never two.

**4. The body's early warning**

The protagonist's body knows before her mind does. The skin at the back of the neck. The throat that closes without a reason she can name. The urge to turn around before there is anything to turn around for. The stomach's refusal to unknot. These signals arrive before the threat is visible — and they are correct.

Write this as the reader's first signal of threat. The body's early warning appears before any objective evidence of danger. The protagonist registers it, rationalizes it away, and proceeds. The reader doesn't proceed with her — the reader stays at the body's signal. This is the gap that creates horror: the reader knows the body is right; the protagonist has decided the body is wrong.

The technique requires the protagonist to be wrong once about her own body before she learns to trust it. The first body signal she dismisses should be followed by the event that proves it was correct. After that, her body signals carry weight for the reader — and when she dismisses the third or fourth one, the reader's dread is at maximum.

Gap between body-knowledge and mind-rationalization: write it in real time. She notices the signal. She names it to herself. She produces the rational explanation. She accepts the explanation. The reader does not. The paragraph ends on her decision to proceed — and the reader is already behind her, watching.

### Fear Pacing Across a Full Novel

| Chapter range | Expected fear beat |
|---|---|
| Ch 1–3 | The ordinary world, intact. Establish what she values, her routines, her people. One unexplained wrong detail in the final scene — small, easy to dismiss. She dismisses it. |
| Ch 4–7 | More wrong details; no pattern visible yet. First body signal. First close encounter — protagonist survives by luck or ignorance, not skill. One false alarm followed by a real event. |
| Ch 8–(N-4) | The threat is real and she knows it. First cost — something she valued is lost. The rules of the threat become partially clear, which reveals that surviving will require something she does not currently have. Dread register sustained. |
| Ch (N-3)–(N-2) | Direct confrontation. The nature of the threat is mostly visible. Terror register. She has used or exhausted most of her options. The impossible thing she must do becomes clear. |
| Final 2 chapters | The horror reveal — the full nature of the threat, the last and worst detail. Either defeat, survival, or escape — each costs something specific. The cost must be real; an ending where she survives and loses nothing is not horror. |

In a 14-chapter horror novel, the first cost should arrive no later than chapter 8. Readers who reach chapter 10 without losing anything they cared about begin to believe the story will not keep its threats. Horror's credibility depends on following through.

### Applying to Parallel Writing (Horror)

When spawning agents for parallel chapter writing in a horror book, pass each agent its fear arc position:

```
Fear arc position: Ch-{N} of {total}
Current fear register: {unease / dread / terror / horror}
What the protagonist knows about the threat: {specific — what she has seen, heard, lost}
Who has been lost or compromised: {list — the cost tracker}
What resources remain: {allies, safe spaces, options still open}
Threat nature revealed so far: {what has been shown vs. withheld}
Next fear beat this chapter: {body signal / partial reveal / false alarm → real event / first cost / direct confrontation}
```

Agents without this context will default to sustained dread with no escalation — the register will flatten. Always provide the current position and the next beat.

## The Reveal Architecture — How Much to Show

This is horror's central craft problem. The threat is most frightening when partially seen.

**First appearance:** effect only — sound, shadow, the aftermath of its presence. The protagonist knows something was there. No direct description. The reader constructs.

**Second appearance:** one specific wrong detail — the angle that is impossible, the number of fingers, the way it smells. Still not full description. This detail should be genuinely specific and genuinely strange — specific enough to be real, strange enough to be wrong.

**Third appearance:** more — one additional detail, selected for maximum wrongness. Save the worst for last. The worst thing about the threat should arrive at maximum tension.

**Full reveal:** at or after the climax. Some horror should withhold explanation entirely. Decide whether your threat *improves* on revelation or *diminishes* on it:
- Creature horror: usually improves — the thing in the dark is often better seen
- Psychological horror: usually diminishes — the explanation reduces the threat to a mechanism
- Cosmic horror: always withholds — by definition, full revelation is impossible
- Domestic horror: the reveal is the person, not the monster — name the person; withhold the why

### The Monster Problem

Every monster has a reveal tipping point: too early and the reader's imagination is replaced by something lesser; too late and the withholding becomes frustrating rather than frightening.

Rules:
- The threat's *effect* must escalate even when the threat itself is not fully shown — the body count or the cost must rise, the protagonist's options must narrow, the reader must feel the threat growing even in chapters where it does not appear
- Each partial reveal should add one genuinely terrible specific — not general menace, but a particular wrong thing that the reader will remember
- The worst thing about the threat should be the last thing the reader learns — not physical description but what it *means*: what it wants, what it does to the ones it takes, what knowing it costs the protagonist

## Genre-Specific Scene Techniques

### The Isolation Architecture

Horror requires that the protagonist's access to help, exit, and allies be systematically reduced across the narrative. The reader must feel this reduction as inevitable — not as the author removing resources, but as the protagonist's own reasonable choices adding up to no exit.

The sequence: communication is cut first (phone, internet, the one person who would believe her). Then physical mobility (car, distance from town, weather, injury). Then allies are reduced — not all at once, but one per act, each loss costing the reader something they were invested in. Finally, options inside the threat's radius are closed until the protagonist faces the climax with only what she has always had: herself.

The critical craft requirement: each step in the isolation should follow from a reasonable decision. She drives to the remote location for a reason. She loses the phone in a way that makes sense. She trusts the person who is going to cost her because that person has been trustworthy. The isolation should be traceable back to a chain of rational choices — the horror is that rational choices led here.

Isolation also applies to psychological resources: who believes her, who she can speak the truth to, whose perception of her is uncorrupted by the threat. As these narrow alongside her physical options, the loneliness of the horror position becomes part of the genre's emotional content.

### The Unreliable Senses Scene

Horror depends on the protagonist being in some doubt about her own perception. Use one unreliable senses scene per act — a moment where neither the protagonist nor the reader can be certain what happened.

For the ambiguity to work rather than feel like cheating, two conditions must hold: first, there must be a physically possible explanation that does not involve the threat — something that could account for what she experienced. Second, the explanation must be slightly less likely than the threat explanation — the reader should be able to believe it, but shouldn't quite.

Physical evidence that reads both ways: the mark on the wall could be old paint or it could be something else. The voice could have been a dream or it could have been real. The door that opened could have been the wind or it could not have been.

The psychological horror of perception failure is not the uncertainty itself — it is what follows from it. If she cannot trust her senses, she cannot make reliable decisions about threat. She cannot know when to run. She cannot know whom to trust. The unreliable senses scene doesn't just create doubt about what happened; it undermines her decision-making capacity for all subsequent scenes.

How to resolve ambiguity: only resolve it when the resolution makes the threat *worse*, not better. If she concludes it was the wind, the reader remains at maximum dread — because the reader doesn't believe it was the wind. If the resolution confirms the threat, the fear register escalates. Never resolve ambiguity in the protagonist's favor in a way the reader accepts — this deflates the scene's accumulated tension.

### Domestic Horror Technique

Domestic horror is the most disturbing sub-genre because it places the threat inside the space that is supposed to be the definition of safety. The horror is not that there is something terrifying in the world — it is that the place she should be safest has become the place where the threat lives.

Execution requires sequence: establish the domestic space *before* the horror arrives. The reader must know the kitchen, the bedtime routine, the specific chair she sits in — these details are not atmosphere, they are load-bearing. The horror lands hardest on the reader who has spent three chapters in that kitchen and now watches it change.

The ordinary thing that becomes the horror vector: it should be something she does without thinking. The thing that requires no decision, that is simply part of the texture of her day. The morning coffee. The locked door she checks before sleep. The path between rooms she walks in the dark because she knows it by body. When the threat enters through this — when the locked door is no longer sufficient, when the dark path is no longer safe — the horror is in the loss of bodily certainty in her own home.

Domestic horror often involves a person as well as a place: the partner who is no longer quite the partner, the child who is not quite the child, the neighbor whose schedule has changed in a way she cannot account for. The domestic betrayal — someone who shared her space has become part of the threat — is one of the most effective and most AdSense-safe horror vectors available.

### Cosmic Horror and the Limits of Language

Lovecraftian and cosmic horror require a different approach than any other sub-genre. The threat is, by definition, beyond human comprehension — which means the writer cannot describe it directly. The direct description kills the thing it names.

Three techniques for writing the unwriteable:

*Describe the effect on the mind that perceives it.* The threat itself is not described; the breakdown of the protagonist's language and thought when she encounters it is. She tries to think about it and the thought slides away. She tries to describe it and the description fails. Write the failure of description as evidence of the thing's nature — the gap between what she experienced and what she can say is where the cosmic horror lives.

*Use the protagonist's language failing her.* She reaches for a word and it isn't there. The sentence she starts does not end in the usual way. The paragraph that should describe what she saw instead describes the absence of a description — and the absence is more disturbing than anything she could have named. The reader's imagination, confronted with the space where a description should be, reaches toward it and finds only scale.

*Write the effect on other people.* The person who looked directly at the thing and survived is not intact in the ways that matter. Their speech has changed. Their relationship to ordinary objects has changed. What they can perceive of the normal world has been permanently altered. The protagonist sees this and understands that what the thing takes is not life but legibility.

Cosmic horror's AdSense advantage: it is almost entirely immune to content policy concerns because the threat is never physically described. The horror is philosophical, not anatomical.

## Environmental Drama (Horror)

| Fear beat | Environment type | What it adds |
|---|---|---|
| Unease → dread | Her own home or daily space, changed by one wrong thing | The horror of the familiar violated is more disturbing than any exotic threat; she cannot call this space dangerous because it has always been safe, which means she cannot act on what her body is telling her |
| Dread → terror | Enclosed with no exit (corridor, car, locked room, basement) | The geography denies escape; proximity to threat is inevitable; the walls are the problem as much as what is inside them |
| Liminal threshold | The edge spaces (basement stairs, attic access, the treeline, the door that opens the wrong way, the window at 3am) | The threat is on the other side; the protagonist stands at the membrane; these spaces have only two states — the before of crossing and the after |
| Terror → horror | The place with history (where something happened before) | The past event is the explanation she is missing; the environment knows the answer to what she is facing; the horror is that the answer was always here, embedded in the place she has been standing |
| Isolation complete | Remote location (the house at the end of the road, the winter cabin, the island at low tide) | Distance has been weaponized; the geography is the trap; no help is arriving from outside the frame; the story must resolve with what is already inside it |

## Synopsis Formula (Horror)

| Sentence | Job |
|---|---|
| **1 — The normal world** | One sentence, specific, domestic, not ominous. Name what she values and what her life looks like when it is intact. Do not hint at the horror — the contrast is the hook. The reader must believe in this life before it is threatened. |
| **2 — The intrusion** | The moment the threat arrived. Specific — not "strange things began to happen" but the concrete first moment: what she found, heard, or saw that could not be explained away. This sentence should be strange enough to create a question without answering it. |
| **3 — The nature of the threat** | What it does, not what it is called. Name by effect: "it takes the ones she brings home," "it answers questions she hasn't asked yet," "it has been here longer than the house." The reader should understand what kind of horror this is from this sentence alone. |
| **4 — The structural trap** | Why she cannot simply leave, call for help, or be believed. The trap must be earned — not a contrived phone outage but a real reason the situation contains her. Relationship, obligation, disbelief, or cost — name the specific thing that keeps her inside the problem. |
| **5 — The impossible thing** | Not "defeat the monster" but the specific terrible thing she must do or understand or accept. The harder the thing, the stronger the hook. If the impossible thing is something the reader would also be unable to do, the identification is complete. |
| **Closing beat** | The question the reader cannot answer without reading — not "will she survive" but the specific terrible thing she must confront. The closing beat should make the reader feel the weight of what is coming. |

## Short Form Horror

Horror is one of the genres that works best in short form. Flash horror (under 1,500 words), short horror (2,000–6,000 words), and the horror novelette all work because:

- The unknown threat is sustainable without full revelation — short form can end before the explanation arrives, and the unresolved is a valid ending
- Short horror can end on dread rather than resolution — withholding the explanation is structurally sound in a way it is not in a novel
- The shorter the form, the more effectively the reveal lands in the final lines — compression increases impact

### Short Horror Beat Structure

1. **Wrong thing, already present:** the story opens inside the wrong thing; it is already happening; the reader does not get the before. The first sentence should establish that something is wrong without naming what. The reader catches up; the story does not slow down for them.
2. **The ordinary context:** what the world normally looked like — established quickly and economically, in service of contrast. Two or three specific ordinary details are enough. The reader needs to know what was lost before the loss is complete.
3. **Escalation:** the wrong thing gets more wrong; every attempt to normalize it fails; the logic of the ordinary world does not apply here. Each beat should close one exit and open one new question.
4. **No exit:** the protagonist realizes the expected solution — call someone, leave, explain it away — does not exist in this situation. This beat should feel inevitable in retrospect: the setup led here.
5. **Revelation or confrontation:** what the threat actually is — or a deliberate refusal to name it. Both are valid short horror endings. The refusal to name is only valid if the gap between what she experienced and what language can hold is itself the ending. If the gap is just evasion, name the threat.
6. **The cost:** the final image; what was lost or changed; the reader should feel it rather than understand it. The last sentence of a short horror story is the sentence the reader carries out with them. Make it specific, strange, and exact.

### Short Horror Vocabulary

**Use freely:** `wrong`, `still`, `cold`, `already`, `waiting`, `the sound that stopped`, `the thing she hadn't seen yet`, `before she could name it`, `too quiet`, `exactly where she had left it`, `hadn't been there before`

**Use sparingly — once per story maximum:** `fear`, `understand`, `real` — these words have maximum impact when used precisely once, at the turn; used more often they flatten

**Avoid:**
- `creepy`, `spooky`, `eerie` as generic descriptors — earn the atmosphere with the specific detail, not the adjective
- Gore that is detailed without dread function — description of injury or death that does not produce fear in the reader is not horror, it is forensics
- `Suddenly` as a tension device — if the scene requires "suddenly" to make the event feel abrupt, the scene did not build correctly; cut "suddenly" and fix the preceding paragraph
- Explaining the horror in the final sentence — the ending should produce the feeling of understanding, not the understanding itself
