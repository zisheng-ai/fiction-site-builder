# Genre: Sci-Fi & Dystopian

Load this file alongside `story-long-write.md` when writing science fiction or dystopian fiction.

---

## Sub-genres

| Sub-genre | Core premise driver | Reader promise |
|---|---|---|
| Space Opera | Interstellar scope, political conflict between civilizations or factions | Scale, spectacle, and personal stakes embedded in galactic consequence |
| Hard Sci-Fi | Extrapolation from real physics, biology, or technology with internal consistency | Intellectual credibility — the reader trusts the world follows its own rules |
| Dystopian | A social or political system taken to its logical extreme and lived from the inside | The cost of compliance and the price of resistance; recognition of real-world systems |
| Cyberpunk | Corporate dominance, neural-digital integration, identity under technological pressure | The grit of bodies in a world designed for data; power as infrastructure |
| Biopunk / Genetic Engineering | Modification of life itself — human, animal, ecosystem — and its class and ethical consequences | The body as contested territory; what is human when the definition is purchasable |
| Climate Fiction | Environmental collapse or radical transformation as the story's physical and moral terrain | Grief for what was lost, and what small survival looks like inside loss |
| First Contact / Xenofiction | Encounter with genuine non-human intelligence or consciousness | The vertigo of not being the measure of all things; communication across an unbridgeable gap |
| Post-Apocalyptic | After the collapse — survival, memory of the before, and the question of what to rebuild | Endurance; the value of what was destroyed; who gets to decide what comes next |
| Military Sci-Fi | Combat at technological scale; loyalty, command, and the human body in extreme machinery | Competence under pressure; the ethics of force; the cost paid by the people who fight |
| Progression / System Sci-Fi | Quantified advancement through levels, stats, or visible power metrics in a sci-fi frame | Satisfying escalation; visible progress; earned dominance |

## The Sci-Fi Click Standard

Sci-fi readers buy three things simultaneously: **AN IDEA TAKEN TO ITS LOGICAL EXTREME** (not stopped short at the convenient point), **HUMAN STAKES INSIDE THAT IDEA** (the personal and specific, not the symbolic), and **WORLD-BELIEF** (the reader must believe, for the duration of reading, that this could actually be the world).

A scene that has the idea but no human stakes is a thought experiment. A scene with human stakes in a world the reader doesn't believe is melodrama. Sci-fi requires all three: the idea forces the stakes, and the stakes are only felt in a world the reader trusts.

## Named Emotional States (Sci-Fi)

| State | Internal contradiction | How it reads on the page |
|---|---|---|
| **Cognitive dissonance of the normal** | This world's rules violate everything she was raised to believe is right; they feel completely normal because they have always been the rules | The moment she perceives them as wrong is the story's inciting event — write the sensation of a frame cracking; the specific detail that refuses to be explained away; she is more frightened by her own capacity to have not seen it than by the thing itself |
| **The weight of being right alone** | She has understood something that would change everything if anyone believed her; no one believes her | The burden is not the knowledge but the isolation of knowing; she speaks carefully now, gauging before she says anything that gestures toward the truth; the reader sees the effort of self-editing in every exchange |
| **System blindness** | She cannot see the trap because the trap is the frame she uses to see everything else; the reader can see it clearly; she cannot yet | Write the gap: her explanations for the wrong things are sincere and plausible; give her the best version of the rationalization; let the reader feel the claustrophobia of watching someone think inside the wrong frame |
| **Defiance vs. survival calculation** | Continuing costs too much; stopping costs more; both choices are now losses | She has passed the point where the math comes out clean; she runs the calculation obsessively and it keeps producing the same answer; write that repetition as a physical habit — the count she returns to, the number she can't make come out right |
| **Alien wonder** | Encountering something that is genuinely not human and does not operate on human assumptions; the disorientation is not fear but the particular vertigo of not being the measure of all things | Her reference points fail one by one; she stops interpreting and starts observing; the writing slows to register things without explaining them; the reader feels the expansion of what is possible |
| **Complicity under duress** | She is doing something she knows is wrong because the alternative is worse; she has not stopped counting the cost; she cannot stop | The counting is visible in how she speaks and where she looks; she has developed small private rituals that acknowledge the cost without naming it to anyone; the reader understands the ritual before she explains it |
| **The long view grief** | She can see the pattern; she knows where this trajectory ends; she is the only person in the room who does | The scene is a funeral she has no name for; she is simultaneously in the present conversation and already at its outcome; write the double register — what she says and what she is watching happen beneath what she says |

## The Idea as Character

In sci-fi, the speculative premise — the single changed thing — functions as a structural character. It has agency: it changes what characters can do, what they want, and what it costs them to want it. Every scene involving the premise's consequences is a scene involving this character.

### The Single Extrapolation Rule

Strong sci-fi changes one thing and follows it to its maximum consequence. Not: "it's the future and everything is different." But: "intelligence can be genetically edited, and we follow what that means for inheritance, class, education, love, crime, and memory — in that order of escalation."

The discipline is the craft. Every time the story introduces a second impossible thing for narrative convenience, it dilutes the premise. Ask: does this plot device follow from the single change, or is it a second change I'm introducing to escape a corner? If the latter, find the exit within the premise's own logic.

### Grounding the Speculation

Introduce the speculative element through the ordinary.

> **Wrong:** "The cognitive enhancement drugs, developed by the Neuraxis Corporation in 2041 following the passage of the Enhancement Enabling Act, had become standard prescription medication available to anyone who could afford them."

> **Right:** "She took the cognitive stack with her coffee, the way she'd been taking it since secondary school. The brand had changed twice; the dosage had gone up. Everyone's had. She didn't think about it."

The second version establishes: cognitive enhancement is normalized, has existed long enough to go through brand changes, and has a trajectory of escalating dosage that she treats as background. None of this is stated. All of it is shown through what she treats as routine.

**Rule:** introduce the speculative element through what it is ordinary to do, not through what it is extraordinary to explain.

## Genre-Specific Scene Techniques

### The Inciting Violation

The moment the system reveals its violence. The protagonist has been living inside the system's logic — this is the scene where the system does something that cannot be rationalized away.

- The violation must be specific and concrete: not "the government is authoritarian" but "the officer moved the man out of the queue and no one turned to look"
- The protagonist's first response is likely compliance, avoidance, or flight — not heroism. Write the compliance honestly. The reader needs to understand the pressure that produces it.
- The horror of the inciting violation is its efficiency and its normalcy to everyone but her. Write the contrast between her perception and the room's.

### The Compliance Scene

Before a character can meaningfully resist, she must establish what compliance looks like and what it costs her. The reader must understand the incentive structure of this world — why people comply — before they can understand the cost of refusing to.

Compliance scenes are active scenes. She is not passive — she is choosing survival over integrity, and she knows it. Write that knowledge as internal weight. Every compliance scene should show: the specific cost she is paying, the specific thing she is telling herself to make it bearable, and one small act of resistance that even she doesn't consciously recognize as resistance.

### The Scale Reveal

The moment when the protagonist (and reader) understands that what they thought was the full extent of the problem is a local detail. This is the single most powerful structural tool in sci-fi.

- Plant the full-scale evidence before the reveal — it should be visible on re-read
- The reveal arrives through the protagonist's discovery, not through narration
- The correct emotional response to the reveal is not "how terrible" but "of course it is this large — how did I not see it"
- The scale reveal should arrive at a moment of maximum vulnerability, not maximum strength

### The Resistance Cell Scene

Dystopian fiction's version of the council scene: a group with conflicting stakes and risk tolerances deciding how far to go.

**Dangers:** it becomes an exposition dump; characters speak positions rather than thoughts; the argument feels schematic (the idealist, the pragmatist, the coward).

- Every person in the cell wants to survive and wants the resistance to succeed — but not in the same way and not at the same price. The argument is always about cost, never about goal.
- The protagonist's position in the argument is the scene's real subject: who she is becoming, what she is willing to do now that she wasn't willing to do before.
- The scene ends with a decision. The decision is made under incomplete information. The missing information is in the room somewhere.

### The First Contact Encounter

When writing non-human intelligence that is genuinely alien — not human-in-a-mask — every interaction must fail before it succeeds.

- Communication is not given. Show the failures: the attempts that produce no response, the responses that cannot be interpreted, the moment she realises her interpretive framework is wrong.
- The alien's logic must be internally consistent, even if she cannot yet access that consistency. The reader should feel the coherence before she can articulate it.
- The turning point is not linguistic — it is behavioural. The first genuine communication is a shared action, not a shared word.

## The Resistance Register — Dystopian Hope Arc

For dystopian fiction, the primary escalation axis is the ratio of hope to despair. Manage it as deliberately as romance manages heat.

### Target Hope Level

| Level | Definition | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Compliant despair** | She is inside the system; she has adapted; hope is a liability she has learned not to carry | Opening chapters — this is the ordinary before |
| **Ember** | Something has survived that shouldn't have; it is small and she is protecting it; the system doesn't know it exists yet | The inciting discovery — the moment the story becomes possible |
| **Organized** | The resistance is real and she is part of it; hope has structure and cost; she can now see how much hope costs to maintain | Mid-book — the constructive arc; what is being built |
| **Crisis** | The system has found what she was protecting; what she built is at risk; despair is the rational response; she is not being rational | Late arc — the crisis point; write the specific irrationality of continued hope |
| **Earned possibility** | Not triumph — the possibility that something survives; that the ember is not completely gone; that the cost was not entirely without return | Resolution — honest about cost and honest about what remained |

### Hope Pacing Across a Full Novel

| Chapter range | Expected hope beat |
|---|---|
| Ch 1–3 | Compliant despair as the ordinary world. The system functions efficiently. She has adapted. One small thing survives that she doesn't yet recognize as hope. |
| Ch 4–7 | The ember is found or recognized. She acts on it. The cost of acting becomes visible. |
| Ch 8–(N-4) | Organized hope — the resistance, the plan, the people. Midpoint crisis: the system demonstrates it can reach her even here. |
| Ch (N-3)–(N-2) | Crisis. The system finds what she built. Despair is the rational position. She doesn't take it. |
| Final 2 chapters | Resolution — not necessarily triumph. Earned possibility: one thing survives; the cost is paid; the ending is honest about both. |

### The Dystopian Ending Problem

Dystopian fiction frequently fails its ending by choosing between two wrong options: false triumph (the system is overthrown in a way the book hasn't earned) or nihilism (everyone dies or loses, and the point is that nothing matters).

The correct ending is: **earned possibility with honest cost.** The system continues. One thing survives that the system could not take. The protagonist is changed — not improved, changed. The reader understands what was worth the price and what the price actually was.

### Applying to Parallel Writing (Sci-Fi / Dystopian)

When spawning agents for parallel chapter writing, pass each agent its hope arc position:

```
Hope arc position: Ch-{N} of {total}
Expected hope beat: {compliant despair / ember / organized / crisis / earned possibility}
Premise visibility: {what the reader currently understands about the speculative premise}
Scale of the problem (what the protagonist knows): {local / regional / systemic}
Resistance status (from tracking/threads.md): {what has been built, what has been lost}
Current compliance cost: {what she is doing she knows is wrong}
```

## Environmental Drama (Sci-Fi)

| Emotional beat | Environment type | What it adds |
|---|---|---|
| The system made physical | The infrastructure of the wrong world — compliance checkpoints, rationing queues, monitoring architecture | Oppression is architecture; she moves through it daily and has forgotten to see it; the story is about her learning to see it again |
| The edge of the mapped | The frontier of the speculative world — the last atmosphere boundary, the unarchived sector, the space beyond the wall | Beyond this point, the system's rules do not apply; what replaces them is unknown; the unknown is simultaneously freedom and danger |
| The contaminated familiar | Her own city, her own home, her own body — altered by the premise in a way she has accepted but the reader hasn't | The horror is that she no longer perceives it as altered; write the reader's recognition before the protagonist's |
| The remnant | What the old world left behind — the pre-collapse archive, the pre-system neighbourhood, the pre-enhancement generation | The cost of the premise made visible; the comparison the protagonist cannot fully make because she didn't live the before |
| The inhuman scale | Environments that demonstrate the premise operates at a scale beyond individual response — the orbital view, the continental sensor net, the geological timeframe | The protagonist's smallness is not defeat — it is the correct register for understanding what she is actually fighting |

## Synopsis Formula (Sci-Fi / Dystopian)

| Sentence | Job |
|---|---|
| **1 — The one changed thing** | The speculative premise in one sentence — not "it's the future" but the specific difference and its immediate implication; this is the sentence that tells the reader what kind of story this is |
| **2 — Her position inside it** | Who she is in this world; what role the system has assigned her; what she has accepted that the reader should not accept |
| **3 — The inciting violation** | The specific moment when the system revealed itself as the system — what she saw or did or lost that she cannot un-see |
| **4 — The impossible position** | What she is now being asked to do; both the choice of acting and the choice of not acting are losses; name both losses |
| **5 — The scale she doesn't yet know** | Hint at how large the premise's consequences actually are — the thing the reader will only understand fully later; name the category, not the content |
| **Closing** | The Self-Aware Genre Declaration (formula from `story-long-write.md`): "This is a [genre adjective] [genre noun] about [emotional core 1], [emotional core 2], and [protagonist transformation]." |

## Short Form Sci-Fi

Sci-fi is one of the genres that works exceptionally well in short form because:
- A single speculative premise can be sustained for a short duration without requiring full world infrastructure
- Short sci-fi can withhold the full implication of its premise until the final line — the reader reaches the implication simultaneously with the protagonist
- The best short sci-fi recontextualizes something about the reader's actual world, not just the story world

### Short Sci-Fi Beat Structure

1. **Premise established** — in the first 10%: not explained, but active; she is already inside this world; the reader learns the rules from what she treats as normal
2. **The specific task** — she has a concrete goal that requires the speculative premise to work — or reveals that it doesn't work the way she thought
3. **The complication** — the premise does something unexpected; a consequence she didn't account for; the system reveals a rule she didn't know existed
4. **The implication** — she understands what the complication means — for her, for the system, for the world she's been living in
5. **The ending image** — not resolution; the image that makes the implication felt rather than stated; the reader does the final step

### Short Dystopian Beat Structure

Short dystopian fiction works best as a single day — the day something cracks:

1. **The ordinary day** — compliance as complete routine; the reader sees what normal looks like
2. **The small wrong thing** — one thing doesn't fit; she notices; she immediately tries not to have noticed
3. **The choice** — engage with the wrong thing or walk past it; write the cost of both options as she sees them
4. **The consequence of choosing** — either way, something has changed that cannot be unchanged; the system may not know yet, but she does
5. **The ending** — she is different in a way the system cannot yet measure; the quiet is specific

### Short Sci-Fi Vocabulary

**Use freely:** `ordinary`, `normal`, `of course`, `as usual`, `without thinking` — the mundane of the wrong world is the register that makes the extraordinary land hardest

**Use sparingly (once per story maximum):** `system`, `resistance`, `wrong` — these words carry genre weight; they are most powerful when they arrive after the reader has already felt the thing they name

**Avoid:**
- Technobabble as exposition — earn the term through use before defining it; define it through context, never through parenthetical explanation
- Hope as a stated emotion — show the action hope produces, not the feeling
- Explaining the world instead of inhabiting it — if a character would not stop to explain this to herself, do not stop the scene to explain it to the reader
