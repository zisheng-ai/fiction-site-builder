# Genre: Contemporary Drama / Emotional Fiction

Load this file alongside `story-long-write.md` or `story-short-write.md` when writing family drama, secret identity / hidden lineage fiction, medical drama, or any contemporary emotional fiction where the primary driver is relational tension — guilt, grief, deferred belonging, or identity under pressure — rather than romantic desire.

---

## Sub-genres

| Sub-genre | Core premise driver | Reader promise |
|---|---|---|
| **Family Drama** | Hidden or withheld relationships — illegitimate children, secret siblings, adopted identity revealed — and the fallout of the concealment | The cost of what was kept hidden; who is owed the truth; whether knowing changes anything |
| **Secret Identity / Hidden Lineage** | A character discovers or is discovered to belong somewhere they were never acknowledged — inheritance, bloodline, institutional power | The specific humiliation of the outsider who was always inside; legitimacy as both desire and weapon |
| **Medical Drama** | Hospital setting as moral and emotional pressure cooker — diagnoses that force family reckonings, deathbed revelations, caregiver relationships | The irreversibility of the medical timeline; what people say when running out of time |
| **Workplace / Career Drama** | Professional hierarchy as a site of personal history — mentors who wronged, promotions that cost, promotions that were stolen, colleagues who know the secret | Identity as performance; the gap between professional reputation and private reckoning |
| **Coming-of-Age** | Identity formation under pressure from family, class, or institution — the protagonist is becoming someone; the question is whether it costs them what they love | The specific regret of the person who got what they wanted |
| **Redemption Arc** | A character who did something genuinely wrong seeks either to undo it or to live honestly with having done it | Whether redemption is about the wronged person or the person who wronged them |
| **Second Chance / Reunion** | Characters who separated under unresolved circumstances forced back into contact | What was never said; whether it is too late; what changes if it isn't |
| **Grief / Loss** | Bereavement, absence, and the long aftermath — the story takes place inside the grief, not after it | Being inside loss rather than watching it from outside; the specific texture of mourning a particular person |

---

## The Drama Click Standard

Drama readers click for **emotion they recognize from the inside** — not romantic desire, but the specific sensation of a situation they know the weight of. Every scene must pass this test: *does the reader feel something they couldn't quite name before they read this sentence?*

The three pillars are:
- **Witnessed guilt** — someone knows something about someone else; the knowing changes their relationship to every subsequent interaction
- **Deferred claim** — a character cannot take the thing they are owed without damaging something else; they are waiting for a permission that will not come
- **Earned reckoning** — the moment when what was avoided must be faced; earn it across the full arc; don't shortcut to catharsis

A scene that has all three simultaneously is the structural peak of drama fiction. The story builds toward this convergence.

## Named Emotional States (Drama)

| State | Internal contradiction | How it reads on the page |
|---|---|---|
| **Guilty adjacency** | She is not the one who did the wrong thing; she is the one who knows about it and has benefited by keeping quiet; she did not choose to know | She is careful in the presence of the wronged person — not visibly guilty, but watchful; she gives them small extra courtesies that mean nothing but make her feel marginally less complicit; the reader recognizes this before she does |
| **Performed ignorance** | She knows more than she is showing; she is actively performing not-knowing because the knowing, if revealed, would require her to act | She answers the question in the most literal possible way; she does not ask the natural follow-up; she moves the subject with a gesture that she has convinced herself is natural |
| **Deferred claim** | She has a legitimate claim — on acknowledgment, inheritance, belonging, love — that she cannot make without forcing a confrontation she is not ready for; she has been deferring this claim for so long she no longer knows if she still wants it | Every scene where the claim could be made, she finds the specific reason this particular moment isn't the right one; write the reasoning as entirely plausible; the reader can see the pattern before she can |
| **Witnessed grief** | She is watching someone grieve a person or situation she knows from a different angle; her knowledge makes the grief harder to witness; she cannot comfort them without revealing what she knows | She is very still; she keeps her hands occupied; she agrees with things she knows are not quite true because the true version would break something; write this as a physical economy — she is conserving herself |
| **Inherited wound** | The thing that hurt her was not done to her — it was done to her mother, her father, her grandmother; she carries the injury as a family inheritance; the perpetrator is often dead or reformed or simply not sorry | She cannot quite explain why this matters so much; she is angry on behalf of someone who is no longer present to be angry; the anger is entirely legitimate and entirely without a productive target |
| **Performed belonging** | She is in a room or family or institution where she technically has a place; she does not feel that she belongs; she performs belonging carefully enough that most people cannot tell | The performance is competent and exhausting; she notices the gap between what she says and what she means; in private, or in a scene with one other person who sees through her, the performance slips exactly once |
| **The almost-said** | She almost said the true thing; she stopped herself; what she said instead is technically accurate but wrong in every way that matters | The almost-said hangs in the air between them; the other person doesn't know what to do with it; the reader knows exactly what wasn't said; write the aftermath of the almost-said, not the moment itself |

## Genre-Specific Scene Techniques

### The Overheard Truth

A character overhears information they were not meant to have. The technique's power is not the information itself but what she does with it: she cannot unknow it, she cannot ask about it, and she must now navigate every subsequent interaction carrying the weight of what she heard.

- Write the scene from her position outside the conversation — the half-heard fragments, the inferences, the moment she understands what she is hearing
- The correct emotional response on overhearing is not clarity but dread: now she has to decide what to do with this
- The scene ends not when the overheard conversation ends but when she decides (for now) what she will do — the decision is always provisional

### The Unclaimed Space

A character is in a physical space they are not supposed to be in, or not supposed to feel at home in. The space carries their claim — the family home they were excluded from, the hospital room where they are not on the visitor list, the office where their photograph is not on anyone's wall.

- Describe the space through what is absent for her, not what is present for everyone else
- Give her one specific detail that she registers as both familiar and wrong — the thing that proves she knows this space better than she should, given how little she is acknowledged in it
- The technique is strongest when the other characters do not notice her claim; she is performing normal tenancy in a space she has never been granted

### Parallel Grief

Two characters grieve the same loss from incompatible positions. They cannot fully comfort each other because each one's version of the grief excludes or implicates the other's.

- Avoid scenes where both characters grieve openly at the same time; it resolves the tension before it has earned resolution
- The strongest version: one character is not permitted to grieve visibly — because they are not acknowledged as someone who had the right to the person they lost
- Give each character one gesture that expresses grief that the other misinterprets; the misinterpretation is the scene's engine

### The Performed Normal

A character maintains ordinary surface behavior — family dinner, workplace small talk, a hospital waiting room interaction — while carrying knowledge that makes the normal behavior a sustained act.

- Write the specific texture of the performance: she is present, she is responding, she is even plausible
- Insert one crack: a pause that is slightly too long, a question she doesn't ask that she would normally have asked, a laugh that starts a half-beat late
- The other characters may or may not notice the crack; the reader always does

### Silent Witness

A character witnesses an interaction between two other characters in which both participants believe they are alone. She sees something she was not meant to see — not an overheard truth (which is auditory) but a visible act: a gesture, a concealment, an expression of grief or guilt in an unwatched moment.

- Write what she sees with precision; the act must be ambiguous enough that the reader, like her, isn't entirely certain of the interpretation
- She does not reveal what she saw; she carries it; it changes her relationship to both other characters
- The reveal of what she witnessed (if it comes) is a late-arc scene; the withholding is the technique's duration

## Emotional Tension Register: E1–E5

For drama fiction, the primary escalation axis is the register of emotional pressure. Manage it as deliberately as horror manages dread.

| Level | Definition | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **E1 — Surface normal** | All characters are performing appropriate behavior; the tension is only available to the reader because we have POV access; everything looks fine | Opening chapters; establish the ordinary before the first crack |
| **E2 — Cracks showing** | One or two characters in the scene perceive that something is wrong; they are not naming it; the surface is maintained but visibly effortful | First quarter; the reader is now ahead of most characters |
| **E3 — Partial truth out** | Something has been said or revealed that cannot be taken back; not the central secret, but something adjacent; the relationship must reconfigure around what was said | Mid-arc; a scene-level reckoning that is not the full reckoning |
| **E4 — Confrontation** | The central concealment or deferred claim is directly in the room; the characters must engage with it even if they are not yet fully honest; avoidance is no longer possible | Late arc; the emotional peak before the earned reckoning |
| **E5 — Earned reckoning** | What was deferred is faced; the cost is acknowledged; not necessarily reconciliation, but the truth is now held by everyone it belongs to | Resolution; must be earned by the full E1–E4 arc; any shortcut reads as false |

**Pacing target:** E1 in chapters 1–2; E2 by chapter 4; first E3 by the chapter-⌊N×0.35⌋ mark; E4 at chapter ⌊N×0.75⌋; E5 in the final 2 chapters.

## Environmental Drama (Contemporary Drama)

| Location type | What it carries |
|---|---|
| **Hospital corridor** | The medical timeline is forcing a reckoning; there is a deadline; people say things in hospitals they would not say anywhere else; the setting has its own permission structure |
| **Family home after absence** | The returning character sees the accumulation of time she wasn't there; what changed without her; what was maintained in exactly the same arrangement as if preserving a version of her that no longer exists |
| **The space that proves she doesn't belong** | She is in a room where she has a claim — inheritance, family membership, professional due — and everything in the room demonstrates that the claim is not recognized |
| **Professional setting where personal history intrudes** | She is in her workplace or her field and a personal relationship arrives uninvited; the professional register she uses to protect herself is insufficient |
| **The threshold** | Doorways, waiting rooms, parking lots — transitional spaces where characters are neither inside nor outside the situation; places where someone waits before the thing that will change everything |

## Synopsis Formula (Contemporary Drama)

| Sentence | Job |
|---|---|
| **1 — The hidden thing** | What has been concealed, withheld, or refused — the fact or relationship at the center of the story; state it plainly |
| **2 — Who carries the weight** | The protagonist's position relative to the concealment — she knows, she doesn't know, she was the one kept from knowing, she was the one who kept the secret |
| **3 — The inciting breach** | What forces the hidden thing into proximity with the person it most affects; the event that makes deferral no longer possible |
| **4 — The impossible position** | What she would have to give up or do to face the truth honestly; why both choices cost something real |
| **5 — What is at stake if she doesn't** | The specific damage that accumulates if the truth remains deferred; name it as a relationship, an inheritance, a person's remaining time |
| **Closing** | The Self-Aware Genre Declaration: "This is a [genre adjective] [genre noun] about [emotional core 1], [emotional core 2], and [protagonist transformation]." |

## Short Form Drama

Drama is among the highest-performing short fiction genres for emotional resonance because:
- A single deferred truth can sustain the entire arc without requiring backstory explanation
- The emotional payoff — a single sentence that makes the reader recognize a feeling they couldn't name — is achievable within 8,000–15,000 characters
- Drama short fiction shares best on emotional platforms (Pinterest quote boards, Facebook groups) — the final line is often what gets shared

### Short Drama Beat Structure

1. **The ordinary scene with the wrong detail** — she is in a familiar situation; one small thing is wrong; she is not yet naming it
2. **The thing that can't be explained away** — a second wrong detail, or the first detail reveals itself as larger than it seemed; she is now aware that something is happening
3. **The approach toward truth** — she moves toward the thing she has been avoiding; write the specific resistance she overcomes
4. **The threshold moment** — she is at the point where saying or knowing changes everything; she knows what's on the other side
5. **The reckoning (or the refusal)** — either the truth is faced (E4/E5), or she turns back — and the turning back is the ending; write what the refusal costs her

### Short Drama Vocabulary

**Use freely:** `ordinary`, `as always`, `the way she had for years` — the texture of the established pattern makes the deviation land harder

**Use sparingly (once per story maximum):** `finally`, `always`, `never` — these carry maximum weight; deploy once at the reckoning

**Avoid:**
- Named emotions in the narration (`she felt grief`, `she was guilty`) — externalise through action, gesture, and what she does not do
- Rushing to the confrontation — the power is in the approach, not the confrontation itself
- Resolution that costs nothing — if the reckoning is too easy, the reader doesn't believe it

## Applying to Parallel Writing (Drama)

When spawning agents for parallel chapter writing, pass each agent its tension register position:

```
Tension register: Ch-{N} of {total}
Expected E-level: {E1 / E2 / E3 / E4 / E5}
Named emotional state this chapter: {guilty adjacency / performed ignorance / deferred claim / witnessed grief / inherited wound / performed belonging / the almost-said}
Central concealment status: {unknown to protagonist / suspected / confirmed-but-undisclosed / partially disclosed / fully in the open}
Who currently knows what: {brief summary from tracking/context.md}
Key relationship under pressure this chapter: {which relationship the tension is channeled through}
```
