# Genre: Fantasy

Load this file alongside `story-long-write.md` when writing fantasy fiction. For romantic fantasy with a central pairing, also load `genre-romance.md`. Cover styles are in `cover-styles.md` (Dark Fantasy Illustration register, or British Literary for romantic / historical fantasy).

---

## Fantasy Sub-genres

| Sub-genre | Core reader promise | Narrative engine |
|---|---|---|
| Epic / High Fantasy | A world larger than the reader's, with stakes that match | Quest, prophecy, or world-threatening conflict; the protagonist grows into someone who can face it |
| Dark Fantasy / Grimdark | The world is real enough to hurt; no guaranteed salvation | Moral ambiguity, systemic corruption, survival with compromised integrity |
| Portal Fantasy | The known world as orientation; the new world as transformation | Contrast between the protagonist's original assumptions and the world that breaks them |
| Progression Fantasy / LitRPG-adjacent | Power is earned and visible; growth has a measurable shape | Accumulation of skill, status, or ability; each obstacle is a level; the reader tracks the number |
| Romantic Fantasy | The relationship is the quest; the world frames it | Romance arc inside a fantasy world; the two arcs must reinforce each other, not compete |
| Cultivation Fantasy (xianxia) | Power is infinite if you are willing to suffer for it | Sect hierarchy, breakthrough moments, rivalry, and the cost of reaching the next stage |
| Paranormal / Urban Fantasy | The impossible exists inside the recognisable world; it has always been there | Discovery (protagonist learns the truth) or insider (protagonist already knows; reader learns through her) |
| Historical Fantasy | A real era with one impossible thing that changes everything | The historical setting grounds the reader; the impossible element is the engine |
| Fae / Faerie Court | Beauty and danger are the same thing; the rules protect you only if you know them | Bargains, obligation, true names, and the price of any gift |

## The Fantasy Click Standard

Fantasy readers buy three things simultaneously: **WONDER** (this world is larger and stranger than the reader's world, and that largeness feels real), **STAKES** (something enormous can be lost or won — something the reader cares about), and **WORLD-BELIEF** (the world feels real enough that its rules matter).

A chapter with only world-building is description. A chapter with only action in a world that feels like a stage set has no weight. A chapter that earns all three makes the reader feel that the world would continue to exist even if they stopped reading.

**Non-romantic fantasy does not require desire.** The click standard for epic fantasy is Wonder + Stakes + World-Belief. For romantic fantasy, add desire. For dark fantasy, swap wonder for dread (they operate on the same axis in opposite directions — both convince the reader that this world is more real than the one they're reading from).

## Named Emotional States (Fantasy)

Use these as the scene's throughline. Every action, beat of dialogue, and internal observation should be filtered through the named state.

| Named state | Internal contradiction | How it reads on the page |
|---|---|---|
| **World-scale wonder** | She is small in front of something vast; the vastness is the point, not the problem; she doesn't want to look away even though she knows she should | Movement slows to catalogue detail; internal monologue reaches for comparisons and finds nothing adequate; she stops explaining and starts listing what she sees |
| **Burden of the chosen** | The thing that makes her necessary is also the thing she would give back instantly if she could; she carries it; she does not let herself stop to feel the weight of carrying it | Flat pragmatic exterior; she discusses the task in logistics, not feeling; the weight appears only in what she doesn't say and what she does when she thinks no one is watching |
| **Exile's belonging** | She fits in neither world completely; the old world no longer has a place for her; the new one has not accepted her; she lives in the gap and has stopped waiting for that to change | She watches group interactions from the edge; she translates between worlds in her head without being asked; competence as the only credential she carries |
| **Grief that reshapes** | She has lost something that made the world make sense; the world still exists; it has changed its geometry around the absence; she keeps turning toward where the thing was | Automatic reach for what is no longer there; the world is navigable but slightly wrong-shaped; she does not mention the absence directly but organises everything around it |
| **The magic user's cost** | She can do the thing; the thing will cost her something she cannot afford; she is calculating whether to pay; the calculation has already been made and she hasn't admitted it yet | Deliberate pause before she acts; she names the practical reasons not to; her hand moves anyway; the cost arrives and she does not let herself be surprised by it |
| **Battle resolve** | She has passed through fear and arrived somewhere cold and functional; her body knows what to do; her mind is somewhere slightly above her, watching | Short declarative sentences; sensory detail becomes precise and narrow; the things that would normally distract her have gone quiet; she is not brave — she has simply run out of room for anything but the next action |
| **Impossible loyalty** | She has chosen someone she knows she shouldn't; the choice is already irreversible; she has stopped trying to justify it even to herself | She does not explain her choice when challenged; the loyalty is visible in what she does, not what she says; when others question it she gives the practical answer, not the true one |

## World-Building Economy

The core craft challenge of fantasy prose: the reader doesn't know this world, and you cannot explain it to them directly.

### Show the World Through What Characters Take for Granted

World-building as subtext: what characters assume, do automatically, or find unremarkable teaches the world more efficiently than description.

**Wrong approach:**
> "The city of Varenthis had three rings of defensive walls, constructed during the Consolidation Wars when the Soral Empire defeated the twelve independent city-states three hundred years ago, imposing the ring tax system that persisted to the present day."

**Right approach:**
> "She took the third gate — the quick route, if you didn't mind the smell from the tanners — and paid the ring tax without counting. Varenthis still charged Consolidation tolls. Some things only ended on paper."

The second version establishes: multiple wall rings, a named historical war, an imperial defeat of city-states, a tax that predates living memory, and a protagonist who is local and slightly cynical about her city's politics. None of this is stated. All of it is shown through what she treats as routine.

**Rule:** if you find yourself explaining world-building, convert it to subtext. Ask: *what does a character who grew up here take for granted?* Write that.

### The Magic Reveal Protocol

Introduce magic through consequences, not rules:

1. **First encounter** — the reader sees what magic *does*: the effect, the aftermath, the cost on the caster's face. They understand power without understanding its rules.
2. **Second encounter** — one rule becomes visible through failure or limitation. A cost. A prerequisite. A thing the magic cannot do. The reader builds the system from observed edges.
3. **Third encounter** — the rules that matter for the plot are now implicit. State rules only when the plot specifically depends on them.

Never open with a magic system lecture. The reader has not yet committed enough to the world to receive it.

### The "What Everyone Knows" Technique

Exposition delivered as cultural assumption:

- *"Everyone in [place] knew that [world fact]"* — use sparingly, once per section maximum; it signals that the writer is narrating rather than showing
- Character treats something as routine that the reader finds remarkable — the routine reaction is the world-building
- Dialogue corrects a misconception — the correction teaches without lecturing

## Genre-Specific Scene Techniques

### The Council Scene

Multiple characters with conflicting stakes debating a world-level decision. Fantasy's most dangerous scene type — it easily becomes an exposition dump or a schematic argument between typed positions.

Rules:
- Every character in the council wants something that is **not** the stated topic. The surface debate is cover for the real negotiation.
- The protagonist's position in the room is a scene in itself: who trusts her, who fears her, whose eyes she avoids.
- The council ends with a decision. The decision is wrong. The wrongness is not obvious when the decision is made — it becomes clear later.
- Write one character who says the thing no one else will say. She is either right and dismissed, or wrong in a revealing way.
- The scene's dramatic function is not to inform the reader of the plan. It is to show the protagonist that she is alone.

### The Battle Chapter

Scale is the battle chapter's core problem: too much happening everywhere, none of it intimate enough to feel.

Rules:
- Follow one character's direct sensory experience. No omniscient overview of troop movements. Zoom in.
- Alternate between macro (the battle is turning) and micro (the next three seconds in front of her face).
- The battle must reveal character. What does she do when the plan collapses, when she is alone, when the rules of combat fail? The battle's function is **character revelation**, not action description.
- Name the physical cost specifically. Named injuries. Named losses. The aftermath chapter is as important as the battle itself.
- A battle chapter without a cost is a fantasy sequence. A battle chapter with a cost is a story.

### The Cost Scene

When magic or power exacts its price, write the cost as it arrives — not as a later summary.

If she used power in chapter 8, the cost lands in chapter 8 or opens chapter 9. Never *"she was still weakened by what she'd done in the previous chapter."* Always write the cost as an active, present experience.

Write the cost as three things:
1. The specific physical sensation of it
2. What it takes from her that she needed for the next step
3. One thing she now cannot do that she was counting on being able to do

### The Prophecy Problem

Prophecy pre-commits the plot and strips the protagonist's agency. Use it only with these constraints:
- The prophecy is wrong about at least one thing the protagonist believes it says
- The mechanism of fulfillment must not be the obvious one
- The cost of fulfilling it must be absent from the prophecy — the prophecy does not know what it costs

The prophecy should function as a misdirection, not a spoiler. It should make the reader think they know the shape of the story; the story should be a different shape than the prophecy implied.

### The Ally Separation Scene

When the protagonist loses her primary ally (death, betrayal, mission divergence), the scene has two jobs: the event itself, and the protagonist's reckoning with what she is without that support.

Write the separation in two acts:
- **Act 1 — the event:** quick, specific, with one sensory detail she will carry. Do not linger in this act.
- **Act 2 — the aftermath:** longer, slower, written in the register she uses to suppress feeling. What she does immediately after is more revealing than what she feels. The reader understands the loss by watching her function in it.

## The Wonder Register

Fantasy's primary escalation axis. Manage it as deliberately as romance manages heat or mystery manages dread.

### Target Wonder Level

| Level | Definition | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Grounded** | The world feels lived-in and specific; the reader accepts it as real; wonder is implicit in texture and detail; nothing impossible has been shown yet, but the world is ready to contain it | Opening chapters; establishing the ordinary world before the impossible enters it |
| **Encountered** | Something genuinely impossible appears for the first time; the protagonist's direct experience is the reader's entry point; write her encounter, not the thing itself | First inciting event; the reader has committed enough to receive the impossible |
| **Scaled** | The reader understands for the first time how large this world or this threat actually is; what they thought was the full picture was a corner of it | Mid-book; the protagonist (and reader) thought they understood the scope; they did not |
| **Sublime** | The protagonist stands before something so large or so strange that ordinary human registers fail; what she feels is not awe or fear — it is the sensation of being the wrong scale for what she's perceiving | Late arc; the climax or the revelation that the climax depends on |

### Wonder Pacing Across a Full Novel

| Chapter range | Expected wonder beat |
|---|---|
| Ch 1–3 | The world feels real and specific. One impossible thing held in reserve or glimpsed at a distance. Grounded wonder. |
| Ch 4–8 | First direct encounter with the impossible. Encountered wonder. The rules of this element become partially visible through use. |
| Ch 9–(N-4) | The scope of the world or threat becomes larger. Scaled wonder. What the protagonist thought was the full picture is a corner. |
| Ch (N-3)–final | The sublime appears. The world reveals its largest fact — the thing the protagonist and reader have been approaching without knowing it. |

In a 15-chapter fantasy, the first impossible encounter should land no later than chapter 5. Readers who reach chapter 7 having seen only setting description begin to wonder if the story is actually fantasy.

### Applying to Parallel Writing (Fantasy)

When spawning agents for parallel chapter writing in a fantasy book, pass each agent its wonder arc position:

```
Wonder arc position: Ch-{N} of {total}
Expected wonder beat this chapter: {grounded / encountered / scaled / sublime}
World facts established so far (from tracking/threads.md): {list of world-building elements already revealed}
Magic system visibility: {what the reader currently understands about the magic rules}
Next world reveal (from outline): {what this chapter adds to the reader's understanding of the world}
Current protagonist status (from tracking/context.md): {what she has and what she has lost}
```

Agents without this context will default to neutral world description and will not escalate wonder deliberately. Always provide it.

## Environmental Drama (Fantasy)

| Emotional beat | Environment type | What it adds |
|---|---|---|
| **Stakes become real** | The ruins of what was — the broken city, the dead god's bones, the sealed empire — the past loss made physical | The reader sees what is at stake by seeing what has already been lost; the present threat is no longer abstract |
| **Wonder encounter** | The impossible vista — the floating mountain, the sea of living glass, the forest that moves — presented as simply *there* | The protagonist's first experience is written in micro-beats, not description; write her body before you write the world |
| **Power shift** | The antagonist's space, where his rules apply and hers do not | The power imbalance is architectural and geographic; she is on territory that does not recognise her |
| **The threshold** | The literal boundary between worlds or zones — the treeline, the city gate, the fold in space | Crossing is irreversible; the story knows this before the protagonist does; write the moment before crossing, not the crossing itself |
| **Isolation that clarifies** | Remote terrain — mountain pass, open sea, dead plain — where the external noise is gone | The protagonist is stripped of context; what remains is what she actually is; the empty landscape makes the internal stakes visible |

## Synopsis Formula (Fantasy)

Use Formula K below. For romantic fantasy, follow with relevant romance formula elements from `genre-romance.md`.

#### Formula K — Fantasy Synopsis

| Sentence | Job |
|---|---|
| **1 — The world in one sentence** | Not geography — the single rule or reality that makes this world the only place this story could happen; the premise of the world, not the biography of the protagonist |
| **2 — Her position in it** | Who she is, why she matters, and what she wants that is specific and human-scale — not "save the world" but the concrete thing beneath that |
| **3 — The inciting force** | What has entered her world or been revealed to her that makes the path she was on impossible |
| **4 — The price** | What she will have to sacrifice or become — the internal price, not the external obstacles |
| **5 — The impossible thing** | The specific contradiction at the heart of this story that makes it distinct from every other quest narrative |
| **Closing** | Self-Aware Genre Declaration: "This is a [genre adjective] [genre noun] about [core 1], [core 2], and [protagonist transformation]." |

## Short Form Fantasy

Epic fantasy is difficult in short form because world-building takes space and trust. Sub-genres that work in short form:

- **Portal fantasy** — the protagonist arrives from a known world; world-building is replaced by contrast and her reaction to it; her known-world assumptions become the reader's orientation
- **Dark fantasy / gothic fantasy** — atmosphere carries the world; the world is felt rather than explained; less infrastructure required
- **Slice of a fantasy world** — a self-contained story inside an established-feeling world; the reader accepts the world-rules as given and focuses on the human situation

### Short Fantasy Beat Structure

For portal and dark fantasy short form:

| Beat | Content |
|---|---|
| **1. Entry** | Into the wrong version of a world, or a world with a wrong thing in it; immediate, not preceded by setup |
| **2. Discovery** | What is different here; one rule of this world established through use, not through explanation |
| **3. The specific want** | A concrete goal — find someone, retrieve something, survive the night, understand what just happened |
| **4. The cost** | Achieving the goal costs something the protagonist did not calculate into her plan |
| **5. The consequence** | She returns changed or does not return; the change is the story's point |

Short fantasy must earn the world quickly. If more than 20% of the story is establishing the rules, the story is too short for its world. Default to the felt world over the explained world — if she accepts the impossible thing as real, the reader will too.

### Short Fantasy Quality Checks

- [ ] The world is established through action and assumption — no dedicated world-building paragraphs in the first 15%
- [ ] The impossible element appears by the 25% mark at the latest
- [ ] The protagonist has a concrete want, not a thematic purpose
- [ ] The cost is specific, not symbolic
- [ ] The final image conveys the change without stating it
